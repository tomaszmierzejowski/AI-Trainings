# Day 1 Audio Script: The Engineering of Intelligence

Welcome to Day 1 of the Business AI Masterclass. I want to be clear from the start: this is not a hype course. This is an engineering course. I am a senior software developer, and I have spent years building systems that use these models in production. What I have learned is that the difference between a chatbot that frustrates users and a system that delivers reliable value is not the model. It is the engineering. The prompts. The parameters. The architecture. By the end of today, you will understand how large language models actually work under the hood, why they sometimes fail in predictable ways, and how to control them with the precision of someone who has debugged them at scale. You are business users, but you are here because you want to go deeper. You want to know the why, not just the how. We are moving from magic to math. So let us begin.

What does it mean to understand an LLM? It means knowing that when you type a question and hit enter, you are not having a conversation. You are sending a sequence of integers into a mathematical function. The function returns a probability distribution. A sampling process picks the next integer. That integer maps back to a token. The token becomes a word. The loop repeats. There is no consciousness. No understanding in the human sense. Just computation. And yet, that computation produces text that feels coherent, helpful, sometimes profound. The gap between the mechanism and the experience is where both the magic and the mistakes come from. Our job today is to close that gap.

Imagine you are standing in a crowded cocktail party. The room is loud. Dozens of conversations overlap. And yet, somehow, you can focus on one person's voice. You tune out the rest. Your brain decides, moment by moment, which sounds matter and which do not. That ability, that selective attention, is not magic. It is a computational process. And in 2017, a team of researchers at Google published a paper that would change everything. The paper was called "Attention Is All You Need," and the authors were Vaswani and his colleagues. They introduced the Transformer architecture, and with it, the concept of self-attention. Here is the key insight: when a language model processes your prompt, it does not read it word by word like a human. It looks at every word simultaneously. It calculates, for every pair of words in the sequence, how relevant they are to each other. The word "it" in the sentence "The animal did not cross the street because it was too tired" gets linked to "animal" through attention weights. The model learns these relationships from billions of tokens of training data. Modern models run multiple attention calculations in parallel, what we call multi-head attention. GPT-4 uses dozens of heads; each one might learn to track different relationships, syntax in one head, semantics in another, coreference in a third. And because the Transformer processes all tokens at once, it has no inherent sense of order. So we inject positional encodings, learned or sinusoidal, to tell the model where each word sits in the sequence. This is the architecture that powers ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and every frontier model you have heard of. It is not magic. It is math.

But here is what matters for you as a business user: an LLM is fundamentally a next-token prediction engine. It does not "know" facts. It does not look up answers in a database. It calculates the probability of the next token given everything that came before. Every time you send a prompt, the model is essentially asking: given this sequence of tokens, what is the most statistically likely thing to come next? That probabilistic nature is the source of both its power and its fragility. When the model is confident, it picks the obvious next word. When it is uncertain, it might pick something surprising, creative, or wrong. Understanding this is the first step to controlling it.

Before we go further, I want you to pause and reflect. Close your eyes if it helps. Think about the last time you used an AI assistant and got a response that felt off. Maybe it was a hallucination: a statistic that sounded plausible but was wrong, a date that was off by a year, a citation to a paper that does not exist. Maybe it was a format failure: you asked for JSON and got prose, you asked for a table and got a bullet list. Maybe it was a logic drift: the model gave the right answer for step one but forgot it by step three. Whatever it was, hold that moment in your mind. Now consider: that was not a bug. That was the model doing exactly what it was designed to do, which is to predict the next token based on probability. The question is not whether the model will sometimes be wrong. The question is whether you can engineer your prompts and your parameters to make those failures rare and predictable. That is what we are building toward today. Every concept we cover will have a direct line to something you can do differently on Monday. Architecture, sampling, tools, prompts, advanced techniques. Each one is a lever. By the end of the day, you will have a full toolbox.

Let us talk about tokens. This is one of the most underappreciated concepts in AI literacy. You might think that language models see words. They do not. They see integers. Every piece of text is first converted into a sequence of token IDs through a process called tokenization. The most common scheme is Byte Pair Encoding, or BPE. Here is how it works: common words like "apple" or "hamburger" often map to a single token. Rare words, compound words, and technical terms get split. "Unfriendliness" might become "un," "friend," "li," "ness." Numbers are particularly tricky. The string "12345" might be split into "12" and "345," or even into individual digits. This is why language models struggle with arithmetic when you ask them to compute in their head. They do not see numbers as numerical values. They see them as token sequences. And every token costs money. At typical API pricing, a thousand-word document is roughly thirteen hundred tokens. When you paste a fifty-page contract into a chat window, you are burning tens of thousands of tokens. This is not abstract. This is your budget. There is another subtle trap: the context window. GPT-4o handles about a hundred and twenty-eight thousand tokens. Claude goes to two hundred thousand. Gemini stretches to one or two million. But here is the catch. Research has shown that LLMs suffer from what we call "Lost in the Middle" syndrome. They attend most strongly to the beginning and end of the context. Information placed in the middle gets less attention. So if you stuff a hundred-page document into the prompt and put your critical instruction in the center, the model might miss it. Place your most important instructions at the absolute start or the absolute end. Use structured delimiters, XML tags or markdown headers, to help the model navigate. So the next time you wonder why your prompt is expensive or why the model lost track of something in the middle of a long document, remember: tokens are the atomic unit, and position matters. Count them. Respect them.

So we have tokens going in. What comes out? The model does not output words directly. It outputs a vector of raw scores called logits. There is one logit for every token in the vocabulary, which for modern models is around a hundred thousand. These scores are then passed through a function called Softmax, which converts them into probabilities that sum to one hundred percent. So after Softmax, you might get something like: "The" has a forty percent chance, "A" has a thirty percent chance, "Apple" has a fraction of a percent. The model is essentially rolling a weighted die. Even when "The" is the most likely option, there is still a chance it picks "A" or something else. That randomness is built into the math. And that is where our next topic becomes critical.

We need to talk about controlling that randomness. The primary lever is Temperature. Technically, Temperature is a scaling factor that divides the logits before they pass through Softmax. When you set Temperature to zero, you are telling the model to always pick the highest-probability token. No dice roll. No creativity. Deterministic. This is what you want for code generation, for JSON output, for any task where consistency matters more than variety. When you set Temperature to one, you are using the raw probability distribution. The model might pick the second or third most likely word. That creates natural variation. When you set Temperature to one point five or higher, you flatten the curve dramatically. Words that had almost no chance suddenly become viable. The output becomes creative, surprising, and sometimes incoherent. Think of it like a slot machine. Low Temperature means the machine is rigged to always give you the jackpot. High Temperature means every symbol has a fair shot. There is a subtle point here: Temperature does not control creativity. It controls randomness. True creativity in an LLM comes from good prompting: diverse examples, clear role assignment, open-ended task definitions. Temperature just adds noise. Sometimes that noise produces a surprising metaphor. Sometimes it produces gibberish. For business applications, you almost always want the machine rigged. Reliability over creativity.

But Temperature is not the only knob. There is Top-P, also known as nucleus sampling. Instead of considering the entire vocabulary, you sort all tokens by probability and take only those whose cumulative probability reaches a threshold, say ninety percent. So if the top few tokens already account for ninety percent of the probability mass, you ignore the rest. This dynamically shrinks the pool when the model is confident and expands it when the model is uncertain. There is also Top-K, which simply takes the top K tokens regardless of probability. And there is Min-P, a newer technique from 2025 that scales the threshold with the top token's probability. When the model is very confident, Min-P keeps the pool small. When it is uncertain, the threshold drops, allowing more diversity. Min-P has been shown to outperform Top-P on both quality and diversity benchmarks. There are also frequency and presence penalties, older mechanisms that reduce the probability of tokens that have already appeared, forcing the model to avoid repetition. And a word of caution: even with Temperature set to zero, you are not guaranteed one hundred percent reproducibility. GPU floating-point non-determinism can cause slight drift between runs. For true reproducibility in critical systems, use seed parameters where your provider supports them. The point is: you have multiple levers. Learn them. Use them. Your production systems will thank you.

Consider a few real-world settings. For code generation, use Temperature between zero and zero point one, Top-P around zero point nine five. You want correct syntax, not a creative variable name that breaks the build. JSON output should always use Temperature zero, ideally with structured output mode so the API enforces valid syntax. For brainstorming, use Temperature between zero point seven and zero point nine, Min-P around zero point zero five. You want diverse ideas but not nonsense. For customer service drafts, use Temperature around zero point three, Top-P around zero point nine. Enough variation to sound human, enough constraint to stay on policy. One size does not fit all. A pitfall to avoid: stacking Top-P and Top-K without understanding their interaction. If you set Top-K to fifty and Top-P to zero point nine, the effective pool is the intersection of both constraints. You might end up with fewer tokens than you expect. Another pitfall: using high Temperature for factual retrieval. You are mathematically increasing the chance of hallucination by flattening the distribution. Less likely and potentially fabricated completions become viable. For any task where correctness matters more than creativity, keep Temperature low.

In the lab, you will run an exercise we call the Slot Machine. You will take the same prompt and run it five times with Temperature one, five times with Temperature zero. You will see the diversity collapse. You will see the output become deterministic. Then you will try different Top-P and Min-P settings and observe how the effective vocabulary pool changes. This builds intuition. When you are back at your desk, tuning a production system, you will know which dial to turn. Pause and reflect again. If you are building a customer service chatbot, what Temperature would you choose? What about a creative writing assistant? A code generator? A fact-checking system? The answer is different for each. There is no universal setting. The senior developer's skill is knowing which dial to turn for which task. We will practice this in the lab.

Pause for a moment. We have just covered the entire pipeline: input becomes tokens, tokens flow through the Transformer, the model outputs logits, Softmax converts them to probabilities, and sampling picks the next token. That loop repeats until the model generates an end-of-sequence token or hits a length limit. Every word you see from an LLM is the result of that loop. There is no other mechanism. No secret sauce. Just math. With that foundation, we can zoom out.

Now we need to zoom out from the math and look at the landscape. The year is 2026, and the idea that ChatGPT is the only AI is dangerously outdated. Different models have different architectures, different context windows, different pricing, and different strengths. Using the wrong tool for the job is one of the biggest productivity killers in software development and business operations today.

Let me give you a quick tour. Claude, from Anthropic, is the undisputed champion of coding and deep reasoning. It is optimized for needle-in-a-haystack retrieval across massive context windows, up to two hundred thousand tokens. If you need to reason through a long document or refactor complex code, Claude is often the best choice. The trade-off is cost and occasionally stricter safety alignments that can trigger false refusals. Gemini, from Google, offers something different: a Mixture of Experts architecture with context windows of one to two million tokens. It processes video, audio, and text natively in the same latent space. If you need to analyze a two-hour video or a massive codebase in one shot, Gemini's context window is unmatched. ChatGPT and GPT-4o remain the versatile generalists, optimized for ultra-low latency and human-like voice interaction. They are fast, capable, and widely integrated. Then there are the specialized tools. Cursor is not just a chat interface. It is a VS Code fork that maintains a vector embedding of your entire local codebase. It uses an internal multi-agent loop to plan refactors, locate dependencies across files, and apply diffs directly. For coding tasks that span many files, Cursor is in a class of its own. And Perplexity is different again. It is not a foundation model. It is an orchestration engine. It does not have a single set of weights. It has a pipeline: search, scrape, embed, retrieve, synthesize. The LLM is the last step in a chain. The chain is the product. Can Perplexity hallucinate citations? Yes. If it cannot find the answer, the synthesizer might invent a URL that looks real. Always click the citation. Always verify. But for tasks where the answer exists on the web and you need it attributed, Perplexity is the right tool. You give it a query, it searches the live web, scrapes the top sites, and uses an LLM to synthesize results with citations. Cursor with Claude. A quick creative brainstorm? ChatGPT. A two-hour video analysis? Gemini. Data leakage usually happens when employees use free consumer interfaces instead of the enterprise API. Know your procurement. Know your data boundaries.

The lesson here is model routing. Do not use one tool for everything. Use the right tool for the right task. A codebase migration? Cursor with Claude. Regulatory compliance search? Perplexity. Quick creative brainstorming? ChatGPT. Massive document analysis? Gemini. Pricing matters too. We have covered the physics and the landscape. Now we get to the interface: prompting. This is where most people fail. They treat the AI like a colleague they can chat with casually. "Fix this." "Make it better." "Give me a list." The model does its best, but without structure, you get generic, inconsistent, unusable output. The solution is to treat prompting like programming. You are writing instructions in English that a probabilistic engine will execute. And the best framework for that is RTF: Role, Task, Format.

Let me show you the difference. A bad prompt: "Fix this rule." The model has no context, no format, no constraints. It produces something generic. A good prompt using RTF: "Act as a senior Pega architect. Refactor this Java step to use a Data Page. Return the new code block and a list of three unit tests. Do not use PublicAPI." Same model. Dramatically different output. The RTF framework gives the model everything it needs to succeed.

Role assigns a persona. When you say "Act as a senior Pega architect," you are priming the model's vocabulary distribution. The model activates reasoning patterns and terminology from its training data associated with that role. Task starts with an active verb. "Draft," "Refactor," "Classify," "Summarize." Specificity is critical. "Write an email" produces something generic. "Write a three-paragraph denial email citing the Flood Exclusion clause, tone empathetic but firm" produces something you can actually use. Format defines the output shape. JSON, CSV, markdown table, numbered list. Without format specification, the model defaults to conversational prose, which is rarely what production systems need. And finally, constraints. "Do not use jargon." "Do not exceed one hundred words." "Do not include personally identifiable information." These are your guardrails. Together, Role, Task, Format, and constraints transform a vague request into a deterministic specification. Research shows that structured prompts like this reduce regenerate loops by fifty percent or more and produce outputs that are copy-paste ready. Same model, same weights, same intelligence. The only difference is the quality of the instruction. One more pitfall to avoid: "Make it professional" is too vague. What does that mean? Fix it with specificity: use eighth-grade reading level, no jargon, formal tone. "Give me a list" is missing format. Fix it: give me a markdown table with columns for Issue, Severity, and Fix. And never use real personally identifiable information in your few-shot examples. Always use synthetic data: Jane Doe, Policy DEMO-001. Your prompts will end up in logs, in version control, in shared cookbooks. Treat them as code.

There are two basic modes of prompting: zero-shot and few-shot. Zero-shot means you ask the model to perform a task with no examples. It works well for simple, well-defined tasks. It fails on complex or ambiguous ones. Few-shot means you provide two to five examples of input and output before the actual task. The model mimics the pattern. This is critical for enforcing specific JSON schemas, teaching domain-specific classification labels, or establishing tone and style. The mechanism is simple: few-shot examples are processed by the attention mechanism. The model attends to their structure and replicates it. Why does this work? Because the Transformer was trained on vast amounts of text that contained patterns. When you show it a pattern explicitly, you are aligning its attention with your intent. You are saying: this is the shape of the output I want. The model has seen similar shapes before. It will complete the pattern. The more precise your examples, the more precise the completion. More examples mean a stronger pattern signal, but also more token cost. For most business use cases, two to three examples for simple patterns and five to seven for complex schemas is the sweet spot. More than ten usually wastes tokens with diminishing returns. And one more distinction: system prompt versus user prompt. The system prompt sets persistent behavior. "You are a helpful assistant that always responds in JSON." The user prompt is the per-turn input. In most implementations, the system prompt carries higher attention weight. Use the system prompt for persona, format rules, and constraints. Use the user prompt for the specific task. Never duplicate instructions in both. And never put secrets in your system prompt. System prompts can be extracted via prompt injection. Assume they will leak. Design accordingly.

But we are not done. Basic prompting relies on the model's immediate statistical intuition. For complex enterprise workflows, multi-step fraud detection, mathematical auditing, or deep code refactoring, immediate intuition fails. We need to give the model a scratchpad. We need to serialize its computation. And that brings us to Chain-of-Thought.

In 2022, researchers at Google, including Wei and his colleagues, published a paper that would change how we prompt. The paper was called "Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models." It was one of those rare research contributions that immediately crossed into practice. Within months, prompt engineers were adding "Think step-by-step" to every complex query. The results were so consistent that some models now have Chain-of-Thought baked into their system prompts for certain task types. But understanding the mechanism matters. It is not a magic phrase. It is a structural intervention. You are changing what tokens the model generates, and therefore what tokens it can attend to when generating the next one. The insight was simple: language models have no internal working memory. They calculate the next token based only on the tokens that came before. So if you ask a model to multiply 342 by 912 in its head, it fails. But if you give it paper, it succeeds. Ask a human to do that multiplication mentally, and they will struggle. Give them a pencil and paper, and they will work through it step by step. Chain-of-Thought is the paper. It is the scratchpad. It is the working memory that the model does not have internally but can create by writing tokens into its own context. By prompting the model to "Think step-by-step" or "Show your work," you force it to generate intermediate reasoning tokens. Those tokens get appended to the context window. The model then attends to its own logic before predicting the final answer. It is effectively serializing computation, giving itself working memory through its own output. The results were dramatic. Chain-of-Thought increased accuracy on reasoning tasks by forty percent or more in the original research. For a mortgage approval workflow, zero-shot might hallucinate a debt-to-income calculation. Chain-of-Thought forces the model to extract gross income, extract monthly debts, calculate the ratio, compare to the threshold, and only then give the verdict. Accuracy can jump from sixty percent to ninety-eight percent. The cost is tokens. CoT generates three to ten times more output tokens than zero-shot. For a routing task, zero-shot might use fifty output tokens; Chain-of-Thought might use three hundred. At ten dollars per million output tokens, that adds up at scale. So use zero-shot for simple tasks: summarization, translation, classification. Reserve Chain-of-Thought for tasks that require multi-step reasoning. And there is a powerful combination: Few-Shot Chain-of-Thought. Provide examples that show the reasoning process, not just the answer. "Here is a problem. Here is how I thought through it step by step. Here is the answer." The model learns the pattern and replicates it. Few-Shot CoT is among the strongest prompting techniques we have. For complex, high-stakes decisions, Chain-of-Thought is often non-negotiable.

There is an even more advanced technique: Tree of Thoughts. Imagine you are solving a hard problem. Instead of following one reasoning path, you generate multiple candidate next steps. You evaluate each path. You abandon the unpromising ones. You expand the winner. That is Tree of Thoughts. It is inspired by breadth-first and depth-first search algorithms from classical computer science. The LLM generates several "thoughts," critiques each, picks the best, and continues. This cannot be done in a single prompt. It requires orchestration code, Python scripts, API loops. You generate options, rate them, proceed on the best path. It is powerful for complex coding, strategic planning, and tasks where a wrong turn early leads to a dead end. Tree of Thoughts is the frontier of prompt engineering. The paper came from Yao and colleagues in 2023. The title says it all: "Tree of Thoughts: Deliberate Problem Solving with Large Language Models." It cannot be done in a single API call. You need a loop. Generate three candidate next steps. Use the model to rate each one. Pick the best. Expand from there. If you hit a dead end, backtrack. This is how humans solve hard problems. We consider options. We reject bad paths. We explore. Tree of Thoughts gives the model the same capability. We will touch on it in the lab, but the key takeaway is: for the hardest problems, one shot is not enough. You need to explore.

There is also decomposition. Break monolithic tasks into sequential sub-tasks. "Write a banking app" becomes "Define the SQL schema," then "Write the API routes," then "Write the frontend." Each sub-task gets its own optimized prompt with focused context. You cannot do decomposition in a single prompt. Asking the model to plan and write everything in one response exceeds output limits and degrades quality. You need orchestration: multiple API calls, passing the output of step one as input to step two. You manage the state. The model does not remember across calls unless you give it the context. And there is iterative refinement: pass the output back to the model with a critical persona. "You are a senior auditor. Find three security flaws in the above code." The model self-corrects. Set a maximum retries limit to prevent infinite loops, or you risk the model spinning forever. These techniques, used together, transform the LLM from a one-shot guesser into a deliberate problem solver. And remember: Chain-of-Thought does not make the model smarter. It does not add intelligence. It serializes computation. It gives the model more tokens to work with, a scratchpad to hold intermediate steps. The model was always capable; we are just giving it the right interface.
