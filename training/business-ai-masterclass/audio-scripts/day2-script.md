# Day 2 Audio Script: Building Systems

Welcome to Day 2 of the Business AI Masterclass. Before we dive in, let me orient you. Yesterday we mastered the single prompt. We learned how transformers work, how attention lets the model focus on what matters, and how tokenization breaks your text into pieces that the model understands. Remember BPE, byte-pair encoding? The algorithm that splits words into subword units so that rare technical terms and multilingual text get represented efficiently. We explored temperature and randomness, chain of thought prompting, and the physics of why these systems sometimes hallucinate. You built a reasoning engine. You learned to control the black box. But here is the truth: a single prompt, no matter how clever, is still just a brain in a jar. It has no memory of your corporate documents. It cannot search your knowledge base. It cannot call your APIs or take action in the real world. Thirty to sixty percent of enterprise generative AI use cases in 2026 involve systems that go beyond a single prompt. Today we build those systems.

Our journey has five major stops. First, we will explore the mathematics of memory: how text becomes numbers in a vector space, and how those numbers let us search by meaning instead of keywords. Second, we will walk through the full architecture of Retrieval Augmented Generation, from document ingestion to the final answer, including the production-grade techniques that separate hobby projects from enterprise deployments. Third, we will bridge the gap between chat and action by learning how tool calling and API integration turn an LLM from a conversationalist into an agent that can actually do things. Fourth, we will put it all together in the ReAct pattern, the foundational architecture for autonomous agents published by Yao and colleagues in 2022. Fifth, we will close with a Day 2 retrospective and code review that prepares you for the security challenges ahead. Let us begin.

Imagine you are standing in a vast grocery store. Not a small corner shop, but a warehouse-sized megastore with thousands of products. Every product has a GPS coordinate. Apples are at twelve point three, forty-five point six. Bananas are nearby at twelve point five, forty-five point eight. Bleach is in the cleaning aisle at eighty-nine point one, two point three. When a customer walks in and asks, where is something healthy to snack on, you do not search for the exact words. You convert their question into a coordinate. You find the products whose coordinates are closest. That is vector search. That is the mathematics of memory.

In the world of AI, we call these coordinates embeddings. An embedding is a list of floating-point numbers that represents the meaning of a piece of text. When you run the word king through an embedding model, you get something like zero point one two, negative zero point nine eight, zero point five five, and so on. OpenAI's text-embedding-3-small produces fifteen hundred and thirty-six such numbers. Text-embedding-3-large produces three thousand and seventy-two. Each number is a dimension in a high-dimensional space. More dimensions mean finer semantic granularity, but also higher storage and compute costs. For most enterprise use cases, fifteen hundred thirty-six dimensions are sufficient. Benchmark both on your actual data before committing to higher dimensions. Similar meanings land close together. Unrelated meanings land far apart.

There is a famous example that captures this beautifully. If you take the vector for king, subtract the vector for man, and add the vector for woman, you land almost exactly on the vector for queen. The model has learned relational semantics. It has captured something about gender and royalty that exists in the geometry of the numbers. This is not magic. It is statistical co-occurrence patterns learned from billions of tokens. But the result is that we can perform arithmetic on meaning.

When we want to measure how close two vectors are, we use cosine similarity. Think of it as measuring the angle between two arrows in space. If the arrows point the same direction, the angle is zero and the similarity is one. If they point in opposite directions, the similarity is negative one. Most text pairs fall between zero point three and zero point nine. Mathematically, cosine similarity is the dot product of two vectors divided by the product of their magnitudes. The result tells you how aligned they are, regardless of length. Why cosine and not simple Euclidean distance? Because cosine measures the angle, not the length. A short sentence and a long paragraph about the same topic can have very different vector magnitudes, but their angles will be similar. Cosine similarity gives us magnitude-invariant comparison. It is the standard in production vector databases. Dot product and Euclidean distance are alternatives; most vector databases support all three. But cosine is the default for good reason.

Pause and reflect for a moment. We have just translated human language into a coordinate system where we can run mathematical operations. Every document in your knowledge base can become a point. Every user question can become a point. Finding the answer is now a geometry problem: which points are nearest? This is not teaching the AI anything new. We are using geometry to find the exact paragraph that holds the answer, and then we are giving that paragraph to the AI to read. The AI does not need to have memorized your corporate documents. It reads them at query time. This is the foundation of semantic search. A user asks, how do I fix a slow database? Keyword search returns nothing because no document contains that exact phrase. But embedding search returns a document titled Optimizing PostgreSQL Query Performance, because the vectors are semantically close. The model does not need to have seen your internal documentation during training. You give it the relevant chunks at query time. That is the core insight. Multilingual embedding models like Cohere's embed-multilingual-v3 go further. They map how are you and jak się masz into nearby vectors. You can search across languages without translation. The geometry is universal.

Embedding models are not the same as language models. They are specialized. They only produce vectors, not text. They have no decoder. No autoregressive generation. They read the input and output a fixed-length vector. They are much cheaper and faster. OpenAI's embedding API costs roughly two cents per million tokens. GPT-4o input costs two dollars and fifty cents per million. For retrieval, you use embeddings. For generation, you use the LLM. They are different tools for different jobs. Do not use a generative model for retrieval. Do not use an embedding model for generation.

A few caveats before we move on. Embeddings capture statistical patterns, not true understanding. The phrase the bank of the river and the bank approved the loan may land close together if the model lacks sufficient context. The fix is to embed full paragraphs, not isolated sentences. More context means better disambiguation. Also, embedding quality degrades for highly technical or domain-specific jargon that was not well represented in the model's training data. If your documents are full of proprietary acronyms and internal terminology, consider hybrid search: combining embeddings with traditional keyword search like BM25. We will return to that. And one more thing: if you change your embedding model, you must re-embed everything. Vectors from different models live in different coordinate systems. They are incompatible.

How long does it take to embed a million documents? With OpenAI's batch API, roughly two to four hours for a million documents of around five hundred tokens each. Cost: about ten dollars. The bottleneck is usually chunking and preprocessing, not the embedding call itself. And what about images and audio? Multi-modal embedding models exist. CLIP for images, CLAP for audio. They map different modalities into the same vector space. You can search your image library with a text query. The same geometry applies. When you embed at scale, use the batch API. It is more efficient than embedding one document at a time.

Now we take this mathematics and build a system. Retrieval Augmented Generation, or RAG, solves the two biggest problems with large language models: stale knowledge and private data. The model was trained on a snapshot of the internet. It has a cutoff date. It cannot see your live documents, your customer database, or your internal policies. RAG fixes that. Instead of hoping the model knows the answer, we find the answer ourselves and hand it to the model. The model becomes a reader and a synthesizer, not an omniscient oracle. Why not just fine-tune the model on your data? Fine-tuning changes the model's style and behavior. It does not efficiently add new factual knowledge. It costs tens of thousands of dollars. It creates a model you must host and maintain. RAG is dynamic. Update your documents, re-embed, re-index. Done.

The full RAG pipeline in production has roughly twelve components. You can simplify it to five boxes on a whiteboard: Ingest, Chunk, Embed, Retrieve, Generate. But each box contains nuance. Let me walk you through them. First, document ingestion. You load documents from PDFs, databases, APIs, SharePoint, wherever your knowledge lives. Second, preprocessing. You clean the text, remove headers and footers, handle tables and images. Document parsing libraries like Unstructured or LlamaParse can extract tables as markdown and images as descriptions. You never embed raw PDF bytes. A table in a PDF becomes a markdown table in your chunks. An image becomes a text description if you use a vision model. The goal is to get everything into a format the embedding model can process. Garbage in, garbage out. If your preprocessing drops critical information, your RAG will never recover it.

Third, chunking. This is one of the most critical and underappreciated steps. You must split documents into retrieval units. Too small and you lose context. A clause that spans two chunks will never be retrieved whole. Too large and you inject noise. The model gets distracted by irrelevant sentences in the same chunk. The sweet spot for most use cases is two hundred fifty-six to five hundred twelve tokens. The two main strategies are fixed-size chunking and semantic chunking. Fixed-size chunking means you take five hundred and twelve tokens, with ten to fifteen percent overlap between chunks so that sentences are not cut in half. The overlap is critical. If a sentence spans the boundary between two chunks, the first chunk ends mid-sentence and the second begins mid-sentence. Neither chunk will embed well. Neither will retrieve well. Overlap ensures that important phrases appear in full in at least one chunk. When we say tokens, we mean the same units from BPE tokenization. A token is roughly three-quarters of a word in English. Five hundred twelve tokens is about three hundred eighty words, or a solid paragraph. Fixed-size chunking is simple, predictable, and surprisingly effective. Production data shows it often outperforms semantic chunking on standard benchmarks. Semantic chunking splits on document structure: headers, paragraphs, sections. It creates three to five times more fragments, which increases embedding costs. Use it when document structure is highly meaningful. A law firm indexing fifty thousand contracts might split on section headers so that a termination clause stays intact. A support team indexing help articles might use fixed-size chunks because the structure is less critical. The recommendation: start with fixed-size five hundred and twelve token chunks. Move to semantic only if you have evidence it improves your specific data.

Fourth, embedding. You convert each chunk to a vector using your embedding model. Fifth, indexing. You store those vectors in a vector database. Your options: Pinecone is fully managed, scales automatically, and handles the infrastructure. Weaviate is open-source with hybrid search built in, so you can combine vector and keyword in one query. Qdrant is another open-source option with strong performance. pgvector is a PostgreSQL extension: free, runs in your existing database, perfect for prototyping. For a small team getting started, pgvector is often enough. For millions of vectors and high query volume, Pinecone or Weaviate become necessary.

When a user asks a question, the query side begins. Sixth, query processing. You can use query expansion: generate two or three reformulations of the query to improve recall. The user might ask, why is my refund taking so long? The documents might phrase it as refund processing time or disbursement delay. Techniques like HyDE, which generates a hypothetical answer and embeds that, or multi-query, which runs several phrasings in parallel, help when the user's wording does not match the document wording. Seventh, you embed the query using the same model. Always the same model. Never mix embedding models between indexing and querying. Eighth, retrieval. You search the vector database for the top twenty nearest neighbors. Why twenty? Because the first pass is recall-oriented. You want to cast a wide net. The re-ranker will narrow it. If you only retrieved five, you might have missed the best chunk. Twenty gives the re-ranker something to work with. Ninth, hybrid search. In production, you often combine vector results with keyword BM25 results using something called Reciprocal Rank Fusion. You run both searches. You get two ranked lists. Reciprocal Rank Fusion merges them by combining the inverse of each result's rank. The chunk that appears in both lists gets a boost. The chunk that appears in one list but not the other still gets a score. Vector search handles conceptual similarity. BM25 handles exact terminology. If your documents use specific product codes or legal clause numbers, keyword search catches what embeddings miss. Hybrid search is the 2026 production standard. Do not rely on vector search alone.

Tenth, re-ranking. The initial retrieval gives you twenty candidates of varying quality. Some will be spot-on. Some will be tangentially related. Some will be noise. A cross-encoder re-ranker, like Cohere Re-rank, passes each query-chunk pair through a model that scores them jointly. Unlike bi-encoder embeddings, which score query and chunk separately, the cross-encoder sees both together. It captures nuances like negation and subtle relevance that embeddings miss. Reported precision gains range from eighteen to forty-two percent. You take the top three to five re-ranked chunks. Without re-ranking, the LLM receives noisy context and produces worse answers. With re-ranking, you give it only the best. Eleventh, generation. You inject those chunks into the LLM prompt with a strict instruction: answer using only the provided context. If the context does not contain the answer, say I do not know. The prompt template might look like: Context: [chunk one] [chunk two] [chunk three]. Question: [user query]. Answer using only the provided context. If the context does not contain the answer, say I do not know. The model reads the chunks and synthesizes. It does not reach into its training data. It does not improvise. It answers from what you gave it. Twelfth, evaluation. You measure faithfulness: did the answer stick to the context? Relevance: did it answer the question? And completeness. How do you know if your embeddings are good? Build a small test set of fifty query-document pairs. Measure Recall at ten: does the correct document appear in the top ten results? Aim for above ninety percent. If you are below that, your chunking or your embedding model needs work.

Pause and reflect. You have just built a system that can answer questions from a corpus the model has never seen. In the lab, you will simulate this by hand. Paper RAG. Take a document. Cut it into chunks. Assign keywords to each chunk. Given a question, find the chunks whose keywords match. Read only those chunks. Answer. You just performed RAG manually. The vector database automates the search. The LLM automates the synthesis. But the quality of your chunks determines everything. What if the keywords did not match? That is the semantic gap. Your simplified keywords might miss the connection between the user's question and the document's phrasing. What if the chunk was too small to have context? That is the chunking strategy problem. The paper exercise makes the mechanics tangible before you build the real pipeline. The model does not need to be retrained. You update your documents, re-embed, re-index, and the system has new knowledge. RAG costs roughly ten dollars to embed a million documents once, plus fifty dollars a month for vector database hosting, plus one to five cents per query for embedding and generation. For moderate traffic, you are looking at one hundred to five hundred dollars a month. That is the economics.

One critical misconception: RAG does not eliminate hallucinations. It reduces them. If the retrieved chunks do not contain the answer, the model may still fabricate one. That is why the instruction if the context does not contain the answer, say I do not know is non-negotiable. Always include it in your system prompt.

Consider a customer support knowledge base with ten thousand help articles. A user searches, I cannot log in. Keyword search returns nothing because the articles use phrases like authentication failure or password reset. Vector search returns the correct article because the embeddings capture semantic similarity. The user gets their answer. Or consider a legal team. A lawyer asks, what are the termination clauses for Acme Corp? If you used fixed-size chunking and the clause spanned two chunks, you might retrieve only half. Switching to structure-aware chunking, splitting on section headers, captures the full clause. The chunking strategy is not theoretical. It determines whether your RAG system works or fails.

How often should you re-index? It depends on your data freshness requirements. For a knowledge base that changes weekly, re-index nightly. For static legal documents, once is enough. The beauty of RAG is that you control the data. You are not waiting for a model vendor to retrain. You own the pipeline.

We have given the model memory. Now we give it hands. LLMs are isolated reasoning engines. They can think but they cannot act. They cannot query your database. They cannot call your APIs. They cannot send emails or create cases in Pega. Tool calling, also called function calling, changes that. It transforms the LLM from a text generator into an agent that can request actions on its behalf.

Here is how it works. You define tool schemas. These are JSON structures that describe the available functions, their parameters, and expected types. Every major provider supports this: OpenAI, Anthropic, Google. The schema might specify that get_weather takes a required parameter city of type string, and an optional parameter units of type string with enum values celsius or fahrenheit. It might include a description: Retrieves the current weather for a given city. Use ISO city names when possible. Think of tool schemas as documentation for the AI. The model has no built-in knowledge of your APIs. It reads the schema and infers when to call what. The quality of your tool descriptions directly affects the model's ability to select and parameterize tools correctly. Include clear function names, descriptions, parameter types with constraints, required versus optional parameters, and example values. A poorly described tool will be misused or ignored. A well-described tool will be called at the right moment with the right parameters.

The user sends a message. The LLM reads the message and the list of available tools. It decides: do I respond directly, or do I need to call a tool? If it needs to call a tool, it outputs a structured JSON request. Something like: get_weather, parameters: city, London. The model does not execute anything. It outputs text. Your application code parses that text, validates it against the schema, and executes the corresponding function. Your application code intercepts this. It does not send the JSON to an external server. Your code executes the actual function. It might call a weather API, look up a customer record, or create a case. The result comes back. You feed that result to the LLM as an observation. The LLM reads it and synthesizes a natural language response for the user. Or it decides it needs another tool call. Steps two through five repeat. That is the loop.

Imagine a customer support scenario. A user asks, where is my order? The LLM calls lookup_order with customer_id C-one-two-three. The API returns status shipped, ETA January twenty-eighth. The LLM responds: Your order has shipped and will arrive by January twenty-eighth. Or imagine a Pega integration. An LLM classifies an incoming email as a billing dispute. It then calls create_case with type billing_dispute, priority high, and the relevant data extracted from the email. The human agent sees a pre-populated case, not a raw email. The LLM did not execute anything. It requested. Your Pega API executed. That separation is everything.

The LLM never executes anything. It only outputs a request. Your code executes. This is the critical security boundary. The LLM is the brain. Your API is the hands. The LLM never touches the real world directly. It only asks your code to do things on its behalf. You control what gets executed. You validate inputs. You enforce permissions. You log everything.

Modern APIs support parallel tool calling. When the user asks for the weather in London and the stock price of Apple, the model can output two tool calls in one turn. Your code executes both and returns both results. The model synthesizes a single response. Structured outputs are another advancement. OpenAI's response_format with json_schema and Anthropic's tool use guarantee that the model's output conforms to your schema. No more parsing errors. No more malformed JSON. You define the schema. The model fills it. If the model outputs something invalid, the API rejects it and the model retries. This eliminates a whole class of integration bugs.

A few pitfalls. Do not give the LLM too many tools. More than fifteen and the model struggles to select the right one. Group related tools into categories or use a two-stage approach: first select a category, then select a tool. Always validate tool outputs before feeding them back. If an API returns an error, the LLM may hallucinate a response based on the error message. And never allow the LLM to call destructive tools like delete or update without confirmation. Implement human-in-the-loop for irreversible actions. How do you test tool calling? Mock the tools. Return predefined responses and verify the model's behavior. Build a test suite of twenty or more scenarios covering normal, error, and edge cases. What if the API is slow? The LLM waits. Set timeouts in your orchestration code. If the API takes more than ten seconds, return a fallback message rather than letting the user wait. You might have heard of MCP, the Model Context Protocol. It is Anthropic's standard for connecting LLMs to external data sources and tools. It is a protocol layer on top of function calling that standardizes how tools are discovered and invoked. The concepts we have covered apply regardless.

We have single tool calls. Now we chain them. An agent is an LLM wrapped in a loop. It does not just call one tool and stop. It reasons, acts, observes, reasons again, acts again, until it has enough information to answer. This is the ReAct pattern, published by Yao and colleagues in 2022 in the paper ReAct: Synergizing Reasoning and Acting in Language Models. ReAct stands for Reason and Act. It is the foundational architecture for production agents. Function calling is direct and deterministic: the model picks a tool and calls it. ReAct adds a reasoning step between calls. The model explicitly articulates its thought before each action. That extra step makes the agent more flexible for exploratory tasks. When the path is unclear, when the agent needs to self-correct, ReAct shines.

The loop works like this. The user gives the agent a goal. The agent generates a Thought. I need to look up the customer's order status. Then it generates an Action. A tool call: lookup_order, parameters: order_id, ORD-four-five-six. The system executes the tool. The result comes back: status delayed, reason weather, new ETA February first. That result is the Observation. The agent reads it. It generates another Thought. The order is delayed. The customer will be unhappy. I should check if they have a premium account for expedited shipping. Another Action: check_account, parameters: customer_id, C-one-two-three. Another Observation: tier premium, expedited_eligible true. Now the agent has enough. It generates a final Answer. Your order is delayed due to weather. As a premium member, I have initiated expedited shipping. New ETA January twenty-ninth.

That is the ReAct loop. Thought, Action, Observation, repeat until the agent decides it can answer. The model is the brain deciding what to do. Your code is the body executing the actions. The loop continues until the brain says I have enough information. Under the hood, it is a while loop. While iterations is less than max iterations, generate a thought, generate an action, execute the tool, append the observation to context, check if we have a final answer. If not, repeat. This is a while loop with an LLM inside. Nothing more. Nothing less. In a live demo, you might ask the agent: find the weather in London and recommend what to wear. Thought: I need the weather. Action: get_weather London. Observation: twelve degrees Celsius, rainy. Thought: It is cold and rainy. Answer: Wear a warm jacket and bring an umbrella. Two iterations. The model reasoned, acted, observed, and answered. That is the full loop in miniature.

In the Pod Build lab, you will build a research agent. The workflow: a planner breaks down the user question, a researcher uses Perplexity or search to find facts, a critique step checks if the facts are sufficient, and a writer synthesizes the answer. The twist: if the critique says missing data, you must refine the search and try again. The agent self-corrects. Teams compete on report quality and cost efficiency. The deliverable is a flow diagram, the three prompts used, and one example of the critique step catching a bad result. That last part is crucial. You must demonstrate that your agent can detect when the search failed and recover. The lab scenario might be: find the latest EU AI Act requirements for high-risk AI systems and cite three specific articles. The researcher searches. The critique checks: did we get three specific articles? If yes, summarize. If no, output missing data. If missing data, refine the Perplexity prompt and try again. The critique step is often skipped. Do not skip it. It is what makes the agent robust.

Frameworks like LangGraph provide graph-based state management with a prebuilt create_react_agent. LangGraph is excellent for complex multi-agent systems where one agent delegates to others. LangChain offers higher-level abstractions for common patterns, good for prototyping. Or you can build it yourself with raw API calls in a while loop. Maximum control, minimum abstraction. Whatever you choose, set max_iterations to ten or fifteen. Prevent infinite loops. Set max_retries to two or three for individual tool calls. Log every Thought, Action, and Observation. When the agent produces a wrong answer, you need the full trace to debug. And implement cost tracking. An agent run can make five or ten LLM calls. A five-iteration agent using GPT-4o might consume five thousand output tokens, roughly five cents per run. At ten thousand runs per day, that is five hundred dollars per day. Use cheaper models like GPT-4o Mini for reasoning steps where possible. The economics scale.

What is the difference between an agent and a workflow? A workflow is deterministic. If X then Y. If the order status is delayed, send an email. No LLM in the loop. An agent is probabilistic. The LLM decides the next step. Use workflows for predictable processes. Use agents for ambiguous ones. When the user's intent is unclear, when the path to the answer requires exploration, when you need the model to critique its own work and try again, that is when you need an agent.

Agents can call other agents. This is multi-agent architecture. A manager agent receives a complex task, breaks it down, and delegates sub-tasks to specialized worker agents. One agent searches. Another synthesizes. Another validates. LangGraph supports this natively. The key is constraining each agent's scope. Give it only the tools it needs. In the system prompt, be explicit: you may only use the following tools. If you cannot answer with these tools, say I cannot help with this. How do you prevent the agent from going off-track? Constrain the system prompt. Tell it not to improvise. Not to make up answers. If the tools do not give you what you need, say so. The agent will respect boundaries if you draw them clearly.

Consider a research agent. Given the task, analyze competitor pricing for Q1 2026, the agent might call the Perplexity API to search, receive initial results, then call a critique step: does this cover enough competitors? The critique says, my search only covered three competitors. Let me search for two more. Another search. Another synthesis. The agent self-corrects. A customer support agent receives a complaint. It calls lookup_order, then check_refund_eligibility, then process_refund, then send_email. The entire workflow that took a human eight minutes takes the agent twelve seconds. The loop is the magic. The model is not following a script. It is reasoning its way through.

Pause and reflect. What have we built? We have an LLM that can search a vector database for relevant context, call external APIs to gather live data, chain multiple steps together, and synthesize a coherent answer. It can research, critique its own results, search again if needed, and produce a final report. This is the difference between a five-dollar-per-month productivity tool and a system that replaces hours of manual work. But with power comes responsibility. Full autonomy is for low-risk, high-volume tasks only. Are agents production-ready in 2026? For low-risk, high-volume tasks like customer FAQ and data lookup, yes. For high-stakes decisions like financial approvals or medical advice, only with human-in-the-loop. The technology is capable. The governance is still catching up.
