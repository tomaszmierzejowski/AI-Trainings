Trust, Verification,
and Evidence over Opinion

AI generates. You verify. You own.

Two ways to fail  FRAMEWORK — automation bias

Under-trust wastes. Over-trust damages.

Under-trust

“I checked everything twice, so AI saved me nothing.” The time saving evaporates — and AI gets blamed.

Over-trust

“It sounded confident, so I shipped it.” A fluent guess becomes an organizational fact.

Both are the same error: treating trust as a feeling about the tool instead of a property of the task. Calibration is trainable.

What a hallucination actually is

It’s not lying. It’s completing.

  • A language model predicts plausible text. It does not look facts up.
  • Confidence of tone is a style property — it carries zero information about truth.
  • Fabrications cluster where specificity is required: names, numbers, dates, citations.
Think: a confident intern with excellent grammar. You’d still check the numbers.

Rule 1

Truth lives in systems of record

AI

Understanding, synthesis, drafting.

Systems of record

Databases, source code, controlled documents, official portals — the facts.

Human judgment

Arbitrates between them. Signs the result.

The winning pattern is fusion — never AI alone as a source of record.

Grounding in practice

Three behaviors, cheapest first

  • 1. Paste the authoritative source into the prompt.
  • 2. Use source-grounded tools (NotebookLM, RAG) that answer only from your documents — and cite.
  • 3. Demand citations. Then actually click them.
No citation, no claim — the same standard these slides hold themselves to.

Why it matters  STUDY — Standish CHAOS 2020

The unverified-assumption problem

  • Only ~31% of software projects succeed outright.
  • A dominant cause: decisions made on assumed knowledge of systems nobody fully understands.
  • Most organizations don’t have a knowledge problem. They have an unverified-assumption problem.

High stakes in plain documents

The bid deadline is a high-stakes fact

  • A tender shortlist is high stakes + easy to verify — the portal is right there. So: delegate the search, verify every deadline, budget and eligibility rule at the source.
  • One hallucinated deadline = one missed tender. One misread eligibility rule = a week of wasted proposal work.
  • The 60-second check, in order: dates → amounts → names → conclusions vs. the source.

Verification as behavior, not virtue

Four concrete habits

Citations required

Build it into the prompt. Then click.

60-second spot-check

Names, numbers, dates first — highest fabrication density.

Tests as trust

For code: the suite is the gate. VERIFIED PresoGen AI: ~600 tests across 3 LLM providers.

Second-model check

Reserved for high-stakes + hard-to-verify. The smallest slice of your day.

Governance essentials

Four rules that let you say “yes” quickly

1 · Data

No personal data (GDPR) or client-identifying data into non-approved tools. Ever.

2 · Access

Least privilege for anything an agent can touch.

3 · Injection

External content pasted into prompts is untrusted input — instructions can hide in it.

4 · Trace

Record what AI touched in a deliverable — so review effort can be aimed.

Governance is an enabler, not a brake — four rules that make compliance (and the GDPR auditor) comfortable saying yes in seconds instead of weeks.

Catch the confident lie

  • A polished AI analysis of a small dataset. Three planted errors.
  • A wrong number. A fabricated citation. A subtly inverted conclusion.
  • Debrief question: which error survived longest — and why did it sound right?
