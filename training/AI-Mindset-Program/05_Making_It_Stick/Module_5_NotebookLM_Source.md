# AI-Mindset Module 5 — Making It Stick: The 30-Day Behavior Plan (NotebookLM Source Document)

This document explains how to convert AI training into durable behavior change through deliberate design rather than willpower. It covers why training enthusiasm decays, the individual habit ladder that stacks one behavior per week over thirty days, the social layer of team rituals, leadership modeling, and champions networks, a measurement approach based on leading behavioral indicators rather than sentiment, the anti-patterns that reliably kill adoption, and the structure of the customer-specific application block, including how inferred client assumptions are handled honestly.

## The honest problem: enthusiasm decays, defaults persist

Most corporate training produces enthusiasm that decays on contact with the calendar. After the preceding four modules, knowledge is no longer the gap — participants know more about AI-augmented work than most of their industry. The gap is economic: the old habits are still cheaper this week, and the four psychological frictions named in Module 1 — fear of visible incompetence, identity threat, trust miscalibration, and habit inertia — remain in force. FRAMEWORK (Fogg Behavior Model): a behavior occurs when motivation, ability, and a prompt coincide. Because motivation is the least reliable of the three, the module engineers the other two — making the new behavior tiny (ability) and attaching it to an existing trigger (prompt). The design stance in one sentence: we do not plan to try harder; we redesign the default.

## The individual habit ladder: one behavior per week

The thirty-day plan stacks four behaviors, one per week, each drawn from a preceding module. Week one: a single first-step habit — AI as the first move on one specific recurring task — taken from the participant's Module 1 commitment card and Module 2 habit contract, deliberately small and attached to an existing trigger. Week two: add the verification spot-check from Module 3, sixty seconds prioritizing names, numbers, and dates. Week three: write the first real specification for a task, using the Module 4 template. Week four: review the trail — what did AI touch, what did the participant sign, what does the next month's ladder look like. The ladder's discipline is subtraction: ambition — attempting five habits in week one — is the most common way thirty-day plans die by day four.

## The social layer: rituals, leaders, champions

Individual habits survive when they become socially normal. The module installs three team rituals, of which each team adopts at least one by decision in the room: a sixty-second "AI moment" in the daily stand-up where one person names something they delegated the day before; a shared prompt-and-specification library that lives in the repository and is used, rather than in a wiki graveyard; and a monthly show-and-tell presenting one real workflow including its failures, because failures teach adoption faster than polished wins.

Leadership modeling is the single highest-leverage intervention. Adoption follows visible seniority behavior, not policy: teams do not adopt what leaders mandate; they adopt what they see leaders do. The concrete ask of every leader: once a week, in a channel the team reads, post what was delegated to AI and what had to be corrected. The correction is the active ingredient — visible senior fallibility normalizes verification and dissolves the fear-of-looking-incompetent friction org-wide.

Champions are volunteers, one per team, with explicitly budgeted time, tasked with unblocking colleagues and harvesting wins for the show-and-tell — never with enforcement. FRAMEWORK (Rogers, diffusion of innovations): adoption spreads through trusted peers, not memos. A champion appointed involuntarily, working on top of a full load, holding an enforcement mandate, is not a champion but an anti-pattern.

## Measuring behavior, not sentiment

The module replaces "do you feel more productive?" surveys with leading behavioral indicators, measured monthly and always announced in advance: the first-step rate (the share of eligible tasks where AI was the first step), the verification rate (the share of AI output that received the sixty-second check), specification reuse (rules files and specs in the repository used more than once), and time-to-artifact on one benchmark task, which serves as the first bridge from behavior data to outcome data after two to three months. Outcome metrics such as velocity or revenue lag by quarters and are confounded; behavior metrics lead and are actionable within a month. Two design rules protect trust: everything measured is announced, and metrics evaluate the adoption system, never individuals. These indicators are also deliberately game-proof in a specific sense: gaming the first-step rate requires using AI first on real tasks, which is the desired behavior itself.

## Anti-patterns that kill adoption

Four patterns reliably destroy AI adoption programs. The mandate ("everyone will use AI by Q3") produces compliance theater — usage statistics rise while behavior stays unchanged. Tool sprawl — a new tool every month — produces dabbling and zero habits. The hero — one enthusiast who does everyone's AI work — feels helpful and is a single point of failure; when the hero leaves, the capability leaves in their laptop bag, which is the exact opposite of the fishing-rod philosophy. Silent measurement — tracking behavior without announcing it — collapses the trust on which everything else depends.

## The customer application block and the honesty discipline

The module contains one customer-specific slide applying the habit ladder to the client's actual delivery context. For the program's first external customer — anonymized here as PlatformCo, the Kraków delivery hub of a global enterprise low-code platform vendor — the applied context is low-code platform delivery: intake, workflow configuration, client demonstrations, and presales. The assumed pain points — requirements churn in workflow builds, decision bottlenecks in routing and prioritization, and manual intake effort — are tagged INFERRED, because at the time of writing they derive from pre-engagement analysis rather than from the client's own recorded statements. The delivery protocol requires saying this aloud: the trainer states that the assumptions are the vendor's hypotheses, invites correction, and rewrites the proposals live in the client's language; every correction upgrades an inferred item to a verified one and transfers ownership of the plan to the client. A pre-training survey exists to perform this validation before delivery, and when it has been run, survey quotes replace the assumptions. For any other client, this block is rebuilt from their survey; another client's assumptions are never delivered.

## The close: the rod is yours

The program ends where it began, on the "Daję wędkę" contract. Each participant leaves with a completed one-page thirty-day plan: the Level-3 task, the tool and trigger, the verification rule, the specification candidate, a named accountability partner, and a dated day-thirty review. Each leader leaves with a team sheet: the chosen ritual, the named champion, and three baselined metrics. The program's success criterion is stated in its own terms: if the day-thirty review runs well without the trainer in the room, the training worked — because the deliverable was never the trainer's presence; it was the rod.
