# Module 5 — Making It Stick: The 30-Day Behavior Plan (Trainer Guide)

**Total time:** half-day (≈3 h) · **Leaders REQUIRED** — reschedule rather than run without them; slides 6–7 have no substitute audience · **Materials:** Module 1 commitment cards (collected earlier), Module 2 habit contracts, one-page 30-day plan template, team ritual sheet, day-30 review template, metrics baseline sheet

## Speaker notes by segment

### Slides 1–3 (The problem + the design stance)
Open by returning the Module 1 commitment cards physically — "You wrote this on day one. Raise your hand if you've already done it once." Whatever the count, use it without judgment: high count → "that's motivation; today we make it survive motivation's decay"; low count → "that's normal, and it's exactly why this module exists."
Slide 3: keep the Fogg framing honest and light — it's a labeled framework, not neuroscience theater. The sentence that matters: "We don't plan to try harder. We redesign the default."

### Slide 4 (The habit ladder)
Walk one participant's real example through all four weeks, live, on the flipchart. The ladder's discipline is *subtraction*: one habit per week, and week one's habit must be embarrassingly small. Push back on ambition here — ambition is how 30-day plans die on day 4.

### Slides 5–7 (Social layer — the leadership segment)
Slide 5: have teams pick their ritual *in the room* — an unchosen ritual is a suggestion, a chosen one is a decision.
Slide 6 is the most important five minutes of the module and it is aimed at the most senior person present. Address them directly and warmly. The ask is concrete: "Once a week, in a channel everyone reads, post: 'I delegated X to AI, here's what I had to correct.' The correction part is what kills the fear in the room — it shows verification is normal, not shameful." This operationalizes the Module 1 friction map: visible senior fallibility is the antidote to fear-of-looking-incompetent.
Slide 7: champions must be volunteers with budgeted time. A champion appointed by a manager, on top of a full load, with an enforcement mandate, is the anti-pattern from slide 10 wearing a lanyard.

### Slide 8 (Measurement)
The room will ask for ROI dashboards. Hold the line: outcome metrics (revenue, velocity) lag by quarters and are confounded; behavior metrics lead and are actionable this month. Concede the bridge: "After two or three months of behavior data, we can connect it to outcomes — the benchmark task (time-to-artifact) is your first bridge metric."
Transparency rule: whatever is measured is announced. Silent measurement (slide 10) destroys everything the program built.

### Slide 9 (PlatformCo application block — handle with care)
This slide's honesty IS the sales pitch. Deliver the footer out loud, verbatim: "These assumptions come from our pre-engagement analysis, not from your own words. Correct us." Then facilitate: for each assumed pain point (requirements churn, decision bottlenecks, manual intake — all **[INFERRED]**), ask the room "is this real? What's its actual name here?" and rewrite the slide's habit proposals live with their language.
When corrections come — and they will — visibly welcome them: every correction upgrades an [INFERRED] tag to [VERIFIED] and makes the plan theirs instead of yours. If the pre-training survey has been run by delivery time, replace this slide's assumptions with survey quotes beforehand and say so.
**For other clients:** this slide is the swap-out point — rebuild from their survey; never deliver another client's assumptions.

### Slide 10 (Anti-patterns)
Deliver fast and with humor — each anti-pattern should sting someone slightly. The hero anti-pattern deserves one extra beat: "The hero feels great and helps everyone. And the day they leave, the capability leaves in their laptop bag. Heroes are the opposite of the rod."

### Slide 11 (Working block)
This is the module. Circulate; enforce concreteness with the same standard as Module 1 (reject "use AI more"); check every plan has a *named* accountability partner and a *dated* day-30 review. Leaders complete the team sheet in parallel — ritual, champion, three baselined metrics. Nobody leaves with a blank sheet; a blank sheet today is a silent no.

### Slide 12 (Close)
End on the philosophy, first person, unhurried: "Daję wędkę. I gave you the rod. If the day-30 review runs without me, I did my job." Then — practically — name the follow-up option (day-30 review facilitation) once, without pushing. The program's integrity is that the offer is optional and the plan works without it.

## Discussion prompts
1. "What killed the last change initiative here — and which slide-10 anti-pattern was it?" (often the most revealing conversation of the whole program)
2. For leaders: "What will you visibly delegate to AI this week — and are you willing to post the correction too?"
3. "Which metric from slide 8 would your team game, and how do we design against that?"

## Likely objections & responses
- **"We don't have time for rituals and reviews."** — "The full social layer costs about 90 minutes a month — the daily moment is 60 seconds, show-and-tell is 45 minutes monthly. Compare that to the cost of this training evaporating, which is the default outcome without it."
- **"Measuring behavior feels like surveillance."** — "Two design rules prevent that: everything measured is announced, and metrics evaluate the *adoption system*, not individuals. First-step rate tells us whether the program worked — it is never a performance-review input. Put that in writing for your teams."
- **"Our people will game the metrics."** — "Gaming first-step rate means using AI first on real tasks. That's… adoption. These metrics are chosen because gaming them is doing the behavior."
- **"What if the 30-day plan just doesn't happen?"** — "Then the day-30 review tells you *which* friction won — fear, identity, trust, or inertia — and you rerun the ladder against that specific friction. A failed 30-day plan with a review is diagnostic data. A failed one without a review is just decay."
- **"Can't we just mandate usage?"** — "You can mandate logins; you can't mandate first-step habits. Mandates produce slide 10's compliance theater — usage stats up, behavior unchanged. Visible leadership modeling is slower and it's the only thing that works." (FRAMEWORK-honest: this is field observation + diffusion theory, labeled as such.)

## Failure modes for the trainer
- Running this module without leadership present. The social layer (slides 5–7) has no audience; reschedule instead.
- Letting the working block get talked through instead of written. Artifacts or it didn't happen.
- Overpromising the metrics. Behavior indicators are honest leading signals, not an ROI proof — the program's evidence-over-opinion standard applies to its own success claims too.
- Delivering slide 9's inferred pain points as facts. The [INFERRED] discipline, spoken aloud, is what separates this program from every vendor pitch the client has survived.
