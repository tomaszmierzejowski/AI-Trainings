# Trainer Compendium C1 — Personality and AI Adoption

This file gives the trainer expert-level command of how personality differences shape AI adoption: what DISC and PRISM actually are and how solid they are, what the scientifically validated alternatives say, and a practical profile-by-profile playbook for reading a training room and tailoring the message. The through-line: personality frameworks predict *how people need to be approached about change*, and that is exactly the level at which a trainer should use them.

## DISC — what it is and how honest to be about it

DISC descends from William Moulton Marston's 1928 book "Emotions of Normal People" (Marston, incidentally, also co-created the polygraph and Wonder Woman — a reliable audience laugh that also makes the honesty point). Marston proposed four emotional styles — Dominance, Influence (originally Inducement), Steadiness (Submission), and Conscientiousness (Compliance) — arranged on two axes: task-oriented versus people-oriented, and fast-paced/outspoken versus cautious/reflective. Modern DISC questionnaires were built later by various commercial vendors; there is no single "the DISC test."

The psychometric verdict, stated plainly: DISC instruments generally show weak test–retest reliability (people get different types on retake), many use forced-choice "ipsative" scoring that makes between-person comparison statistically improper, type descriptions exhibit Barnum effects (flattering, broadly applicable statements people readily accept as accurate), and there is little peer-reviewed evidence that DISC type predicts job performance. Professional psychometric bodies do not recommend DISC-class typologies for selection decisions.

So why use it at all? Three reasons. First, familiarity: a large share of corporate audiences have taken a DISC assessment, so it is a shared vocabulary you get for free. Second, the two axes underneath it (task/people, fast/cautious) are real, observable communication-style differences even if the four-box typology is crude. Third, as a *communication lens* — "how should I frame AI change for this kind of listener" — it requires no psychometric validity at all, only face validity. The program's official position, which you should say out loud when using the DISC slide: "DISC is a communication lens, not a diagnosis. It is psychometrically weak, and the validated science underneath is the Big Five. I use it because you know it, and because it makes one true point: the same change lands differently on different people."

PRISM Brain Mapping deserves one honest sentence: it markets itself as neuroscience-based, but independent peer-reviewed validation is thin, and its brain-based framing overstates what the instrument measures. If a client organization already uses PRISM, map its quadrants onto the same two axes and proceed exactly as with DISC — as a lens, never as science.

## The Big Five — the validated backbone

The Five-Factor Model (Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism — OCEAN) is the consensus model of personality in academic psychology: stable across decades, replicated across cultures, measured with normative (comparable) scoring, and predictive of life and work outcomes. Barrick and Mount's landmark 1991 meta-analysis established conscientiousness as the most general predictor of job performance. For technology specifically, research linking Big Five traits to the Technology Acceptance Model (notably Devaraj, Easley & Crant, 2008) finds broadly: **openness** correlates with positive attitudes toward new technology and willingness to experiment; **conscientiousness** strengthens the link between believing a tool is useful and actually using it (conscientious people follow through); **neuroticism** correlates with technology anxiety and lower perceived usefulness; **extraversion** matters mostly via social channels — extraverts respond to what peers visibly do; **agreeableness** shows the weakest and least consistent effects.

Translation for the trainer: openness predicts who volunteers first in the lab; conscientiousness predicts whose 30-day contract survives to day 30; neuroticism (better framed to audiences as "trait anxiety") predicts who most needs the psychological-safety moves in Module 5; extraversion predicts who will amplify — or undermine — the change socially.

## Technology Readiness Index — the domain-specific instrument

If a client asks "can we actually measure our team's readiness?", the defensible instrument is the Technology Readiness Index (Parasuraman, 2000; TRI 2.0, Parasuraman & Colby, 2015) — published, validated, and specific to technology adoption. It measures four dimensions: two motivators — **optimism** (belief technology increases control and efficiency) and **innovativeness** (tendency to be a tech pioneer) — and two inhibitors — **discomfort** (feeling overwhelmed, lacking control) and **insecurity** (distrust, fear of harm). Individuals combine into segments the authors label explorers, pioneers, skeptics, hesitators, and avoiders. The four TRI dimensions map cleanly onto Module 1's four frictions: discomfort ≈ fear of incompetence, insecurity ≈ trust miscalibration, and low innovativeness plus high discomfort together produce habit inertia. This mapping — a validated instrument echoing the program's field-derived friction model — is a legitimate scientific credential for the program; use it when the audience contains an organizational psychologist.

## Oreg's Resistance to Change scale — why resistance is a trait, not a flaw

Shaul Oreg's dispositional Resistance to Change scale (Journal of Applied Psychology, 2003) is the validated evidence that some people are consistently more change-resistant than others across situations — resistance is partly a stable disposition, not stupidity or sabotage. Oreg's four facets are worth memorizing because they explain *why* the same rollout produces four different complaints: **routine seeking** (change costs comfort — "I liked my workflow"), **emotional reaction** (change costs composure — stress and anxiety responses), **short-term focus** (change costs *now* and pays *later* — the adjustment burden dominates even when the change is beneficial), and **cognitive rigidity** (change costs certainty — reluctance to revise views). Notice the direct mapping onto the program's frictions: routine seeking is inertia, emotional reaction feeds fear, short-term focus is the loss-aversion mechanism from Module 1's Kahneman slide, and cognitive rigidity underlies trust miscalibration ("I already concluded AI is useless"). When a leader asks "why do smart senior people resist hardest?", Oreg is the answer: resistance correlates with disposition, and seniority adds identity threat on top — it is not an intelligence problem.

## The profile playbook — reading the room

Use DISC's four boxes as shorthand, silently backed by the science above. For each profile: what AI change *feels like* to them, the early warning sign, and what works.

**High-D (dominant, results-driven, fast + task).** Hears "AI" as: competitive advantage — or a threat to their track record if a rollout fails publicly. Adopts fast when framed as winning: speed, market position, beating the competitor who is already doing this. Their failure mode is *reckless delegation* — skipping verification because checking feels slow. Early warning: they demo AI output they clearly haven't read. What works: the leash rule and the trust matrix framed as risk management ("verification is what protects your results"), Module 4's economics slide (87% discovery-and-analysis cost reduction speaks D fluently), and giving them ownership — a D who "owns the AI initiative" pushes it; a D who was mandated resists on principle. In the lab, pair a D with a C.

**High-I (influential, enthusiastic, fast + people).** Hears "AI" as: exciting, novel, a story to tell. Adopts first and loudest — and abandons first, when novelty fades or the tool embarrasses them publicly. Their failure mode is *tool sprawl and shallow habits*: ten tools tried, none kept. Early warning: they mention a new AI app every week but can't show one finished workflow. What works: the show-and-tell ritual (a stage!), being made champion (Rogers' opinion leader — see C5), the one-habit-per-tool rule delivered with humor, and public recognition for *depth* ("first person to run one workflow for 30 straight days") rather than novelty. An I is your best diffusion asset if channeled and your best chaos generator if not.

**High-S (steady, loyal, people + cautious).** Hears "AI" as: destabilization of a team and routine they have carefully tended — and possibly a threat to colleagues' jobs before their own. Adopts last but most durably: once an S builds the habit, it stays. Their failure mode is *silent non-adoption* — they will not argue in the workshop; they will nod, and change nothing. Early warning: complete agreement with zero questions. What works: the habit ladder (small, safe, incremental steps), an accountability partner (relatedness is their fuel — see self-determination theory in C2), explicit reassurance about what is *not* changing, seeing a trusted peer succeed first (peer proof beats data for an S), and never being cold-called in the room. The Lidka story is written for S listeners: a careful non-technical person who reached full, safe autonomy.

**High-C (conscientious, analytical, cautious + task).** Hears "AI" as: a quality risk pushed by people who don't understand the details. Adopts only on evidence — and one hallucination witnessed live can lock them into permanent rejection (this is Dietvorst's algorithm aversion, C3, embodied). Their failure mode is *analysis paralysis and moving goalposts*: no evidence is ever quite enough. Early warning: they ask for the methodology of every number (excellent!) but never run the tool. What works: this program's entire evidence-badge system (built, frankly, for them), Module 3 as their home turf — evidence tagging, 312 citations, verification protocols — the planted-error exercise (they usually win it, which converts them: the exercise proves verification *skill* matters, and they have it), and framing AI as an instrument that *needs* their rigor rather than replaces it. A converted C is your most credible internal validator; cite their approval to the S's.

Facilitation summary: sell speed to D, stage to I, safety to S, and standards to C — while the underlying program is identical. That is the honest meaning of "personality-aware training": one curriculum, four framings.

## Rogers' adopter curve — the population view

Everett Rogers' Diffusion of Innovations (first edition 1962, fifth 2003) describes how innovations spread through populations: innovators (~2.5%), early adopters (~13.5%), early majority (~34%), late majority (~34%), laggards (~16%). Two trainer-critical implications. First, **the target is the early majority, and they adopt on peer proof, not on evidence or excitement** — which is why Module 5's rituals (visible peer usage) matter more than any statistic. Second, **do not design the rollout for laggards**: converting the last 16% costs more than the first 84% combined, and the attempt (usually a mandate) damages adoption among everyone else. Geoffrey Moore's "chasm" between early adopters and early majority is a practitioner extension (label it as such): the pitch that wins visionaries ("revolutionary!") is exactly the pitch that scares the pragmatic majority ("risky!"). Cross the chasm by changing the story from novelty to reliability — which is what this program's verified-numbers style does.

## Q&A arsenal

**"Is DISC scientific?"** — "As a measurement instrument, weak: low retest reliability, ipsative scoring, little predictive validity — I won't defend it as science. As a communication lens it needs no defense: it reminds us the same change lands differently on different people, and the validated version of that claim is the Big Five plus Oreg's resistance research, which I'm happy to walk through."

**"Should we assess our employees before the rollout?"** — "You can — the defensible instrument is the Technology Readiness Index, not DISC. But I'd invest that budget in the rituals instead: TRI tells you who will struggle; the rituals are what actually help them. Measure behavior (first-step rate), not disposition."

**"Are older employees worse at adopting AI?"** — "The evidence says the strong version of that is a myth. Age effects on technology adoption are small and mostly mediated by exposure, self-efficacy, and habit strength — how long you've run the old workflow matters, and that correlates with age without being age. In the field studies, less-*tenured* people gained most, which is about entrenched habits, not birth years. Design for habit strength, not for age."

**"I'm an introvert / our team is all engineers — does this change the program?"** — "Extraversion changes the *social* channel: introvert-heavy teams need the library and written show-and-tells more than the daily stand-up ritual. The individual habit mechanics are trait-independent."

**"Our people did a PRISM assessment — can we use it?"** — "Use the vocabulary if it helps communication, with the same caveat as DISC: it's a lens. Map its quadrants to the task/people and fast/cautious axes and the playbook applies unchanged."
