# Trainer Compendium C3 — Trust, Algorithm Aversion, and Calibration

This file is the deep science behind Module 3: why humans mistrust and overtrust algorithms in predictable, researched ways; what actually repairs broken trust; and the newest, most uncomfortable evidence on self-perception — people cannot feel whether AI is helping them. Mastering this file lets the trainer handle the two hardest audience members: the burned skeptic and the uncritical enthusiast, using their own psychology, with citations.

## Algorithm aversion — the burned skeptic, explained

The foundational study is Dietvorst, Simmons and Massey (Journal of Experimental Psychology: General, 2015), titled literally "Algorithm Aversion: People Erroneously Avoid Algorithms After Seeing Them Err." Participants forecast outcomes (student performance, airline traffic) and could rely on their own judgment or a statistical model. The model outperformed humans. The key manipulation: some participants watched the model make errors. Result: **having seen the algorithm err, participants abandoned it — even though it remained measurably better than their own judgment, and even when abandoning it cost them money.** Crucially, people tolerate error asymmetrically: a human forecaster's mistake is forgiven ("everyone has a bad day"); an algorithm's identical mistake is treated as disqualifying ("it's broken").

This is the exact anatomy of the "burned once, never again" friction from Module 1, now with a name and a citation. The one visible hallucination that turned your senior expert into a permanent rejector was not an overreaction by a difficult person — it was the textbook human response to watching an algorithm err. Say that sentence to a skeptic and watch the defensiveness drain: their reaction is *normal*, predicted, and — here is the pivot — *correctable by design*.

The correction comes from the follow-up: Dietvorst, Simmons and Massey (Management Science, 2018) found that **letting people adjust the algorithm's output — even trivially — dramatically increased willingness to use it**, and the adjusted usage outperformed pure human judgment. People don't need the algorithm to be perfect; they need to not feel locked out of the loop. The program's translation: every workflow it teaches keeps a human hand on the output (iterate-don't-regenerate, the verification handshake, the leash rule). This is not just safety — it is, per Dietvorst 2018, the *adoption mechanism itself*. Control is the antidote to aversion.

## Algorithm appreciation — the other half of the story

The literature is not one-sided, and a well-read audience member may know it. Logg, Minson and Moore (Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes, 2019, "Algorithm Appreciation") found that laypeople actually weigh advice *more* when told it comes from an algorithm than from a person — across estimates, forecasts, and judgments. The appreciation collapses in two conditions: when people must choose between the algorithm and *their own* judgment (self-confidence crowds out the algorithm), and among **domain experts, who discount algorithmic advice most** — to their own detriment in accuracy terms.

Reconciling the two findings gives the trainer a precise model: default attitudes toward algorithms are mildly positive (appreciation), until either (a) the person watches the algorithm err (aversion kicks in, Dietvorst) or (b) the person is an expert defending their own judgment (discounting, Logg). Now map onto a corporate training room: your juniors arrive appreciative, your seniors arrive discounting, and anyone with a hallucination story arrives averse. Three different trust pathologies, three different fixes: juniors need verification discipline (their risk is overtrust), seniors need the jagged-frontier framing (C4 — the tool complements exactly where their expertise thins), and the burned need restored control (Dietvorst 2018).

## Automation bias and complacency — the uncritical enthusiast, explained

Automation bias is the tendency to over-rely on automated cues, committing two error types (Skitka, Mosier & Burdick, 1999): **omission errors** — failing to notice a problem the automation missed — and **commission errors** — following an automated recommendation that contradicts other available evidence. Parasuraman and Riley's classic framework (Human Factors, 1997) distinguishes automation *use, misuse, disuse, and abuse* — misuse being overreliance (the enthusiast), disuse being underreliance (the skeptic). Decades of aviation and medical research show automation complacency grows with automation reliability: **the better the tool, the weaker the human monitoring** — vigilance decays precisely because failures become rare. This is the scientific basis for Module 3's claim that verification must be a designed habit, not a mood: moods track reliability downward; checklists don't.

The newest and most quotable finding: Lee et al. (CHI 2025, Microsoft Research and Carnegie Mellon, survey of 319 knowledge workers with 936 real generative-AI tasks) found that **higher confidence in the AI was associated with less critical-thinking effort, while higher self-confidence in one's own skills was associated with more**. Critical engagement with AI output is effortful, and trust in the tool is exactly what switches that effort off. The underlying mechanism is cognitive offloading (Risko & Gilbert, Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 2016): we naturally externalize cognition to tools — useful when calibrated (nobody mourns mental long division), corrosive when the offloaded function is judgment itself. The trainer's line: "Trust is the correct long-term goal and the immediate enemy of verification. The 60-second check exists because your vigilance will not survive your satisfaction."

## The METR study — you cannot feel whether AI helps you

The most counterintuitive result in the current literature, and the program's strongest argument for measuring rather than feeling: METR's 2025 randomized controlled trial took 16 highly experienced open-source developers working on their *own* mature repositories, randomized 246 real tasks to allow-AI or no-AI conditions, and measured completion time. Result: **with AI, developers took about 19% longer — while estimating, after the fact, that AI had made them about 20% faster.** A forty-point calibration gap in the population you'd expect to be best calibrated.

Handle this study with the same honesty you demand elsewhere — it has boundary conditions, and knowing them is what makes you the expert. The developers were extreme experts on codebases they knew intimately (years of context the AI lacked); the repos were large, mature, with strict standards; the tooling was early-2025. The study does *not* say "AI slows developers" in general — it says that in the regime where human expertise is deepest and context is hardest to transfer, AI assistance can cost time while *feeling* like it saves time, because accepting fluent suggestions feels like progress. Three program implications. First, it validates the trust-matrix logic: deep-expertise-plus-hard-verification is precisely the quadrant where delegation pays worst. Second, it is the definitive argument for Module 5's time-to-artifact metric: perception is not measurement, even for elite practitioners. Third, paired with Brynjolfsson's novice gains (C4), it completes the honest picture: AI compresses skill gaps from below far more reliably than it extends performance at the top.

## Hallucination, one level deeper

Module 3 teaches "it's completing, not lying." The trainer should hold two levels more. Mechanism: a language model samples each next token from a probability distribution conditioned on context; there is no internal fact database being consulted and no truth-maintenance system flagging fabrication — a plausible-sounding citation and a real citation are generated by the same process, distinguished only by whether the training distribution and context happen to pin down the truth. This is why fabrication concentrates in high-specificity, low-redundancy content: exact numbers, names, references — the places where many continuations are plausible and only one is true. Mitigations, in effectiveness order: **grounding** (put the source in context — the model completes *from* it rather than *about* it; this is NotebookLM's and RAG's design), **citation-and-click** (forces the pin-down to be checkable), **structural constraint** (asking for quotes with page numbers narrows the plausible-completion space), and **cross-model checking** (independent samplers rarely fabricate identically). Also know: hallucination rates are model-, task-, and year-dependent — refuse to quote a universal percentage; any number you give will be wrong within months, and the planted-error exercise is your evidence instead.

## Dunning–Kruger, both directions

Kruger and Dunning (1999): the least skilled overestimate their competence most, because the skill needed to perform is the skill needed to self-assess. Applied twice here. To AI users: novices produce fluent AI-assisted output *and lack the expertise to notice its flaws* — fluency masks incompetence from the very person shipping it; this is why the program insists verification skill (Module 3) must grow *with* delegation (Module 2), never behind it. And reflexively, to AI itself, as an audience laugh with teeth: a language model is in a permanent Dunning–Kruger condition — maximal fluency, zero self-assessment. It cannot know what it doesn't know. That job is permanently yours.

## Q&A arsenal

**"One error and I'm supposed to keep trusting it?"** — "Your reaction has a name — algorithm aversion, Dietvorst 2015 — and it's the normal human response, not a character flaw. The same research found the fix: keep your hands on the output. You don't have to trust it; you have to out-verify it. The people in the study who adjusted the algorithm's output beat both the algorithm and themselves."

**"Experts don't need AI advice."** — "Logg 2019: experts discount algorithmic input the most — and lose accuracy by doing so. And METR 2025 shows elite experts can't even feel the direction of the effect. Expertise makes your verification better and your self-perception worse. Measure."

**"Won't verification skills atrophy if AI does the work?"** — "That's cognitive offloading, and it's a real, researched risk — Lee 2025 found trust in AI reduces critical-thinking effort. It's why verification here is a designed ritual with a checklist, not a vibe. Rituals survive comfort; vigilance doesn't."

**"What's the hallucination rate?"** — "Any single number would be dishonest — it varies by model, task, and quarter. What's stable is *where* fabrication concentrates: names, numbers, dates, citations. That's why the 60-second check starts there."

**"Doesn't the METR study prove AI is overhyped?"** — "It proves something more useful: in the one regime where human context is deepest, AI can cost time while feeling like it saves it. That's an argument for measuring time-to-artifact and for the trust matrix — not against the 26% and 34% gains measured elsewhere. The expert move is holding both results at once."
