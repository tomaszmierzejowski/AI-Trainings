# Trainer Compendium C4 — The Evidence on AI and Productivity

This file makes the trainer fluent in every major empirical study on AI and knowledge-work productivity: design, sample, effect, and — most valuable in front of audiences — the caveats. The program's differentiator is that its numbers survive hostile checking; this file is where that survival is earned. Rule zero, restated: before quoting any figure in print or on camera, re-verify against the original paper.

## The studies, one by one

**Peng, Kalliamvakou, Cihon & Demirer (2023) — the Copilot RCT.** Design: randomized controlled experiment; 95 professional developers recruited online; task: implement an HTTP server in JavaScript; treatment group used GitHub Copilot. Result: treated developers finished **55.8% faster**. Strengths: clean causal design. Caveats to volunteer before a skeptic does: one scoped, greenfield, well-specified task — the regime where AI assistance is strongest; generalization to messy legacy work is exactly what it cannot show. Use for: "the mechanism is real"; never for "expect 56% across your backlog."

**Cui, Demirer, Jaffe, Musolff, Peng & Salz (2024) — the three-firm field experiments.** Design: randomized rollouts of an AI coding assistant at Microsoft, Accenture, and an anonymized Fortune 100 firm; ~4,800 developers; outcome: completed pull requests and related delivery measures. Result: **~26% more completed tasks** on average. The distribution is the story: wide variance; **less-tenured developers gained most** and adopted most; senior developers gained less. Caveats: task completion is not value; code review standards held quality roughly stable but long-run maintainability is unmeasured. Use for: the program's core thesis — same tool, divergent outcomes, behavior as the variable.

**Noy & Zhang (Science, 2023) — professional writing.** Design: online experiment, 453 college-educated professionals, mid-level writing tasks (press releases, short reports, plans); treatment: ChatGPT access. Results: **time down ~40%, quality up ~18%** (blind expert grading) — and **inequality compressed**: weaker writers improved most, and the correlation between a participant's first-task (unassisted) and second-task (assisted) rank dropped sharply. Also notable: many treated participants shipped AI drafts with minimal editing — productivity came largely from substitution, not collaboration. Caveats: short, self-contained tasks; no long-term client relationship or fact-checking stakes. Use for: non-technical audiences (this is *their* study — writing, not code), and for the skill-compression narrative.

**Brynjolfsson, Li & Raymond (2023; QJE 2025) — the call-center study.** Design: staggered rollout of a GPT-based assistant at a Fortune 500 software firm's customer support; ~5,000 agents; outcome: resolved chats per hour. Results: **+14–15% productivity on average; +34% for novice/low-skill agents; minimal for the most experienced**. Mechanism, and this is the beautiful part: the assistant was trained on the behaviors of top performers — it effectively *broadcast tacit expertise downward*. Additional findings: customer sentiment up, employee attrition down, requests for managerial escalation down. Caveats: one firm, one workflow, an assistant purpose-built for the task (not a raw chatbot). Use for: the strongest evidence that AI compresses skill gaps, and for leaders worried about junior performance.

**Dell'Acqua et al. (2023) — "Navigating the Jagged Technological Frontier" (the BCG study).** Design: field experiment with **758 BCG consultants** — elite knowledge workers — randomized to GPT-4 access on realistic consulting tasks. Two task sets: 18 tasks *inside* the frontier (ideation, drafting, persuasion) and one task deliberately designed *outside* it (a business problem whose correct answer required combining spreadsheet data with interview evidence in a way the model would plausibly fumble). Results inside the frontier: **+12.2% tasks completed, +25.1% faster, +40% higher quality** (human-graded); bottom-half performers gained most (+43% vs +17% for top half). Results outside the frontier: consultants with AI were **19 percentage points less likely** to produce a correct solution — the polished, confident, wrong answer problem. The paper also named two effective usage styles: **centaurs** (divide tasks — human does some, AI does others, clean handoffs) and **cyborgs** (continuous interleaving at the subtask level). Caveats: consultants, constructed tasks, one model generation. Use for: everything. This is the single most program-aligned study in existence — the frontier is jagged, knowing its shape is a skill, and inside-frontier gains coexist with outside-frontier damage. It scientifically *is* the trust matrix.

**METR (2025) — the self-perception RCT.** Covered in depth in C3. Sixteen elite open-source developers, 246 real issues on their own mature repos, randomized allow-AI/no-AI: **19% slower with AI, self-estimated 20% faster.** Use for: measurement over feeling; the deep-expertise boundary condition; intellectual honesty theater (an AI trainer citing a negative AI result earns more trust than ten positive citations).

**McKinsey (2023) — "Unleashing developer productivity with generative AI."** Design: internal lab study with ~40+ developers across task types. Results: time roughly halved on documentation, ~35–45% saved on code generation, ~20–30% on refactoring, and **near-zero on tasks rated highly complex** — with gains contingent on training and prompt skill. Caveats: small N, vendor-consultant publication, not peer-reviewed — cite it as directional, badge STUDY but say "industry research, not peer-reviewed" when precision matters. Use for: task-dependence of gains; the training-and-practice dependency.

**Standish CHAOS (2020).** ~31% of software projects fully successful; ~19% failed; the rest challenged. Methodology is proprietary and has academic critics (definition of "success" is contested) — quote the number with its vintage and never extrapolate. The banned number, forever: "73% of modernization projects fail" appears in no Standish report. If someone quotes it at you, that is a gift: "I know that number well — it doesn't exist. Which is a nice illustration of why this program badges its sources."

## The synthesis — what the whole literature says at once

Across every study, gains are a function of three variables, and the trainer should be able to draw this on a whiteboard from memory. **One: task position relative to the frontier.** Inside (drafting, synthesis, scoped generation): large, replicated gains — 26%, 40%, 55%. Outside (deep context, novel integration, exactly-one-right-answer): zero to negative — Dell'Acqua's −19 points, METR's −19%. **Two: user skill baseline.** Gains concentrate at the lower-to-middle baseline (Brynjolfsson +34% novices; Noy & Zhang's compression; Dell'Acqua's bottom-half +43%) because AI broadcasts median-to-good expertise; it lifts floors faster than ceilings. **Three: behavior.** In every study with variance data, workflow-changers captured the gains; tool-havers didn't. The one-sentence version for closing a Q&A: "AI reliably lifts the floor, conditionally lifts the middle, and at the ceiling it helps only those who can map its frontier — which is a learnable skill, and it's what this program teaches."

## Quoting rules (the program's honesty protocol)

State the population before the number ("among ~4,800 developers at three firms…"). State the task regime ("on scoped writing tasks…"). Give the direction of the caveat unprompted — audiences trust the person who attacks their own evidence first. Round conservatively; never compound numbers from different studies into a composite claim. Distinguish measured (a study), verified (our delivered project), and estimated (our projection) — the three words are load-bearing. And when you don't know: "I don't know, and I'd rather tell you that than improvise a statistic" — in a program about verification, this answer *is* a demonstration.

## Q&A arsenal

**"Will AI take our jobs?"** — "The measured evidence so far is task-level, not job-level: studies show specific tasks accelerating — drafting, synthesis, support — and skill gaps compressing. Historical automation reshaped roles more than it erased headcount, but honestly, generative AI's ceiling is unresolved and anyone claiming certainty in either direction is selling something. What is certain: in every study, the people who changed how they work captured the gains. That part is in your control, which is why it's what we train."

**"What ROI should we expect?"** — "I'll give you ranges with sources, not a promise: 14–34% in the best-measured deployment (call centers), ~26% in software field experiments, ~40% time on writing tasks — all conditional on the task being inside the frontier and the habits actually changing. Our own delivered engagements ran around 4× ROI where we estimated it at all, labeled as such. Anyone quoting you a single confident number without a population and task regime attached is quoting marketing."

**"These are all tech companies / consultants — we're different."** — "Partially fair. The call-center and writing studies are the transferable ones — support conversations and documents exist everywhere. What transfers with the least doubt is the mechanism: gains follow behavior change, and the frontier is jagged in every domain. Your specific numbers we'd establish with your benchmark task — measured, not felt."

**"Why do you cite a study where AI made people slower?"** — "Because it's true, and because it teaches the most valuable lesson in the room: elite experts couldn't feel the direction of the effect. If I only showed you the positive studies, I'd be doing the thing this program exists to stop."
