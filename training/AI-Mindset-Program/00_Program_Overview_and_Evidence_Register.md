## 1. Program thesis

Most AI training teaches tools. Tools change every quarter; behavior is what compounds. This program targets the operational psychology of AI adoption — what people do differently on Monday morning — built on the "Daję wędkę" principle: we teach people to fish, we don't build dependency on the trainer. Every claim in the materials is either (a) verified from the author's own delivered projects, (b) a properly cited external study, or (c) explicitly labeled as a framework, estimate, or opinion.

### Tier B — External studies (use only where they speak to behavior change)
- **Peng et al. 2023 (GitHub/Microsoft/MIT RCT):** developers with Copilot completed a scoped task 55.8% faster. Behavioral relevance: the gain required accepting a new interaction pattern, not just installing the tool.
- **Cui et al. 2024 (field experiments, ~4,800+ developers incl. Microsoft):** ~26% increase in completed tasks; largest gains among less-tenured developers; wide variance — adoption behavior, not seniority, predicted benefit.
- **McKinsey 2023 ("Unleashing developer productivity with generative AI"):** up to ~2× on documentation, ~35–45% on code generation, minimal gain on highly complex tasks; gains materialize only with training and practice. Use for: task-decomposition behavior.
- **Standish CHAOS (2020):** ~31% of software projects succeed outright. Use ONLY for the hidden-complexity argument. **Never use "73% of modernization projects fail" — that statistic does not exist in any Standish report.**
