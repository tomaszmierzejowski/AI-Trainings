Every company has an AI hero. The hero is the problem.

You know them. The one person who "gets it." They automate their reports, they draft everything in minutes, colleagues bring them tasks like offerings. Management points at them in town halls: proof we're an AI-forward company.

Here's what the hero actually is: a single point of failure wearing a cape.

The hero feels great and helps everyone. And the day they leave, the capability leaves in their laptop bag. Nothing transferred, because heroism doesn't transfer — it accumulates. Every task the hero absorbs is a task nobody else learned to do the new way.

(My Thursday readers will recognize this: it's the retirement-clock problem from legacy platforms, wearing better sneakers. One person who knows; an organization that depends.)

The fix costs a leader five minutes a week, and I've watched it outperform six-figure training budgets:

Once a week, in a channel everyone reads, the LEADER — not the hero — posts: "I delegated X to AI this week. Here's what I had to correct."

Read that last part again. The correction is the point. It tells every fearful skeptic in the room: the boss's AI drafts aren't perfect either, and the boss says so in public. Fear of looking incompetent — the #1 blocker I wrote about two weeks ago — dies in the light of a senior person modeling imperfection.

Heroes centralize capability. Leaders who show their corrections distribute it.

Does your company have a hero — or a habit? Honest answers only.
