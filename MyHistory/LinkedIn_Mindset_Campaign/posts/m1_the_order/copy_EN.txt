Two people. Same job. Same AI tools. One of them gets 10x more out of it.

I've watched this for a while now in day-to-day work on AI modernization and adoption — and the difference is never where everyone looks for it.

It's not magic prompts. Not technical background. Not age, either.

It's the order.

Person A builds the whole thing alone, then asks AI to fix it up at the end. The report, the offer, the plan — built "the hard way," AI invited in only once it's already done, as decoration.

Person B starts differently. They ask AI for a draft first — then apply their own knowledge, judgment, and taste to it.

Same tools. Opposite sequence. Completely different economics. Person A saves a few minutes at the end. Person B saves the part of the work that always costs the most time and frustration — starting from zero, staring at a blank page.

I keep repeating one rule: AI is your first step to everything. Not the only one. But the first.

The data backs it up. Among nearly 5,000 developers (Cui et al., Management Science, 2024), what predicted the productivity jump from AI wasn't seniority — it was how people fit the tool into their habits. A similar pattern shows up with 758 BCG consultants (Dell'Acqua et al., 2023): those who kept their own judgment over AI's output completed ~12% more tasks, ~25% faster, at higher-rated quality — but outside AI's "jagged frontier," their accuracy dropped.

The tools will change again next quarter. The right order stays with you for good.

So let's be honest: which type are you — the one who starts from an AI draft, or the one who only asks for a polish at the end?
