I plant three lies in a report and hand it to smart people. The most dangerous lie survives almost every time.

It's an exercise from my training sessions. An AI-generated executive summary — fluent, confident, well-structured. Hidden inside: three planted errors. Participants get ten minutes.

Error #1 — a wrong number. The text says a 15% decline; the data says 12%. Found first, almost always. People trust their arithmetic.

Error #2 — a fabricated source. A convincing industry benchmark that simply does not exist. Found second, usually by whoever tries to look it up.

Error #3 — an inverted conclusion. The summary declares the process "healthier than ever" while the underlying numbers got worse every single month. It's decorated with a TRUE statistic as camouflage.

That one survives. Room after room.

Here's why: we're trained to check facts, not framing. A wrong number looks wrong. A wrong conclusion, wrapped in right numbers and confident prose, looks like insight.

Fluency masks falsehood. The best-written paragraph carries the worst error — precisely because good writing switches off our suspicion.

This is the actual AI-trust problem in your company. Not "the AI makes things up" — you already knew that. It's that the making-up arrives in the most persuasive paragraph of the document, holding a real statistic as a shield.

The fix isn't paranoia. It's a 60-second habit — next Tuesday's post.

Until then: would YOUR team catch error #3? Would you?
