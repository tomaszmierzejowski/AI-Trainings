My kids wanted a playhouse. I don't know how to design buildings.

An hour later I was holding architectural drawings. Real dimensions. Structural considerations. A shopping list I could take to the store.

One sentence started it: "My kids want a little house."

I want to be precise about what happened in that hour, because it's not what AI enthusiasts usually claim.

The AI didn't build a playhouse. It didn't replace an architect for anything that matters legally or structurally. What it did was remove a wall I had always accepted as permanent: "I can't even begin, because I don't know how."

I could begin. Beginning was suddenly free.

I checked the load-bearing suggestions against common sense and the lumber against my wallet. I rejected two designs before the third. My judgment stayed in the loop the entire time — it just finally had something to judge.

The playhouse stands in our garden. The kids repainted it a color I'd never have chosen. That part, no AI predicted.

I tell this story more often than any enterprise case study, because everyone has their own "little house" — the thing they never started, because starting required an expertise they don't have.

AI doesn't replace knowledge. It removes excuses for not having it.

What's your little house — the thing you've never started because you "don't know how"?
