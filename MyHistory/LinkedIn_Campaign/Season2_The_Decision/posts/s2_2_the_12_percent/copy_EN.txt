Here's a question your last assessment vendor hoped you'd never ask: "What percentage of the codebase did you actually read?"

I've seen the honest answer for a typical 3-week discovery sprint. It was a small fraction of the total.

Not because the consultants were lazy. Because reading 100% manually is economically impossible — at consultant reading speed, a 2-million-line platform is years of billable time. So the industry standardized on sampling: read the "important" parts, interview the team about the rest, extrapolate.

The problem isn't the low coverage. The problem is what the report did next: it presented conclusions about 100% of the platform, with no coverage disclosure anywhere.

Imagine an auditor who checked 12% of transactions and issued an unqualified opinion on the whole ledger. You wouldn't accept that from finance. IT accepts it every year.

The self-test: pull up your last assessment report and look for three things.

1. A coverage statement (what % of files was scanned — scanned, not "reviewed")
2. Source references (findings that cite files and lines, not interviews)
3. A list of what was NOT examined

Zero out of three is the industry standard. That's not an accusation of your vendor — it's the reason we built a different method.

How many out of three did your last report score?
