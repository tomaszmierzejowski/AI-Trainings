Somewhere in your company there is one engineer who knows where the bodies are buried.

You know the name. You just thought of it.

At one enterprise we analyzed, the platform had run for 20 years. Business logic lived in 17 modules. When we asked who could enumerate what those modules did, the answer was one person.

He was not planning to stay forever.

This is the risk nobody budgets for, because it doesn't appear in any dashboard. It appears in an HR system, as a retirement date.

Documentation doesn't fix it — his knowledge was never written down, and interviews capture what people remember, not what the code does.

Here is what does fix it: reading the code. All of it. In that engagement, full-coverage analysis reconstructed 143 business rules at 94% confidence from roughly 220,000 lines — each rule traced to the exact lines that implement it. The knowledge stopped living in one head.

His retirement went from an incident waiting for a date to a calendar entry.

Two questions, and the first one is rhetorical:

Who is your one person?

And what is your plan for the day their calendar entry appears?

Every number verified. Every finding traced to source code.
