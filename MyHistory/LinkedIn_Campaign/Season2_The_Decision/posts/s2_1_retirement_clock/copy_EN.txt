Somewhere in your company there is one engineer who knows where the bodies are buried. You know the name — you just thought of it.

At one enterprise we analyzed, business logic lived in 17 modules. When we asked who could enumerate what those modules did, the answer was one person. He was not planning to stay forever.

This risk doesn't show up in any dashboard. It shows up in an HR system, as a retirement date. And documentation doesn't fix it — his knowledge was never written down, and interviews capture what people remember, not what the code does.

What fixes it is reading the code, all of it. On another engagement, full-coverage analysis reconstructed 143 business rules at 94% confidence from roughly 220,000 lines — each rule traced to the exact lines that implement it. That's what it looks like when knowledge stops living in one head: a retirement goes from an incident waiting for a date to a calendar entry.

Who is your one person — and what's the plan for the day their calendar entry appears?
