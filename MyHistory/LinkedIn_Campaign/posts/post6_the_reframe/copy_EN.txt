For 14 years, I read maybe 20% of any codebase I assessed. Everyone did — that's what an enterprise assessment was: read what you can, sample the rest, estimate from experience. Nobody called it "20% coverage". We called it discovery.

Enterprise architects ask me one question more than any other: "Doesn't AI hallucinate?" Yes. Constantly. And I still use it on every engagement, because of how the work is actually divided.

AI reads the code: thousands of files, every endpoint, every connection, every data flow. Work that used to take a team of senior consultants 8–14 weeks now takes days — on our engagements, discovery runs about 20x faster than traditional sampling.

Then I read the findings. I check every line reference, separate signal from artifact, and make every trade-off decision. Nothing reaches a client that I haven't verified against the source.

The research points the same way: in a peer-reviewed study of 4,867 developers, AI assistance meant 26% more completed tasks (Cui et al., Management Science). National Australia Bank put 6,000 developers on AI tooling and reported legacy modernization running 3x faster.

But what convinced me wasn't a statistic. It was the first time I finished reading a complete enterprise codebase — not a sample — and understood what was actually in it.

The expertise matters more now, not less. It's just pointed at the whole picture instead of a fifth of it.
